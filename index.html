Nami

Ride the wave

Product Design Challenge by Jimena Zapolski — Light-it 2026

01 — THE BRIEF
Designing for anxiety beyond the therapist’s office
Create a digital experience that helps people manage anxiety in everyday life—especially in moments where professional support is not immediately available.
02 — THE PROBLEM
Anxiety doesn’t wait for therapy — it happens in real life.
Through research, I identified a critical gap: anxiety doesn’t happen during therapy, it happens in between. For young students, existing tools fail exactly when they need help the most.
03 — FOCUS
This isn't about meditation. It's about immediate support.
This project focuses on students who are already in therapy but still experience anxiety spikes. They aren't looking for content; they need something to help them in the moment.
04 — CORE PAIN POINT
"I've tried apps before, but they feel too generic — like they don't really get me."
In moments of anxiety, teenagers lack a familiar and accessible presence they can rely on. There is no immediate, human support when an episode occurs.
05 — SOLUTION
Nami: a real-time companion for anxiety
Nami is a real-time AI companion designed for the exact moment anxiety hits. It learns from the user, understands their needs, and guides them through simple, practical steps, bridging the gap between therapy sessions and real-life moments of anxiety.

Empathize

Research

Understanding the real problem before designing the solution

Before I designed a single screen, I needed to understand what actually happens in the body and mind during an anxiety episode — not what wellness apps assume happens. I spent time in clinical studies, app store reviews, and Reddit threads where real people described what they reach for at 3am. The gap between what research shows and what products offer was the real brief.

Professional data & reports
  • 359Mpeople worldwide had an anxiety disorder in 2021 — the most common mental disorder. Only 1 in 4 who need treatment receive it.
  • In low-income countries, less than 10% receive care vs. more than 50% in high-income countries.
  • +52%increase in anxiety disorders aged 10–24 between 1990 and 2021 — significant peak after 2019.
  • Therapy usually occurs once every 1–2 weeks, leaving long periods without professional support.
  • Digital CBT interventions can reduce anxiety symptoms by 30–50% — but less effective during acute crisis when adherence drops.
  • AI-powered tools reduce symptoms by 30–50%, best as complement to care — not replacement.
  • "The problem is not just the anxiety — it's the lack of support in the 'in-between'."
Actual user behavior during an episode
  • Seek to isolate or escape the triggering environment.
  • Numbing via doomscrolling — TikTok, Instagram, or repetitive games (Candy Crush, Tetris) to saturate the senses and "turn off" intrusive thoughts.
  • Call or write to someone they trust.
  • Symptom checking — Googling palpitations or shortness of breath to rule out heart attack.
  • Try therapy techniques but under high cognitive activation they forget them or can't perform them alone.
  • Physical instinct: pacing, cold water on face, squeezing objects, music, avoidance.
  • Key: The person is not looking to "resolve the anxiety." They want to lower the intensity NOW.
Patient's voice — forums & reviews

"I hate when apps tell me 'close your eyes and focus on your breathing.' If I focus on how I'm breathing, I realize I'm short of breath and the panic attack gets a thousand times worse. I need to open my eyes and look at something."

"Guided meditations are great for Tuesday afternoons when I'm a bit stressed. At 3 a.m., when my heart is racing at 140 bpm, hearing birds and bells makes me feel like the app is mocking me."

"Sometimes I feel overwhelmed out of nowhere and I don't know what to do in that moment."

"The most cited problem across all apps is memory. Users record everything, and the next day the AI has forgotten the most important detail. That's not just a technical bug — it's an emotional breakdown."

"I've tried apps before, but they feel too generic — like they don't really get me."

"When I'm anxious, I don't have the energy to read or go through complex steps."

Market gaps
  • Interface friction: Most apps require unlocking, searching, waiting for splash screen, ignoring promotions — before reaching help. During a panic attack, users don't have fine motor control or patience.
  • Designed for prevention, not crisis: Calm and Headspace require menu navigation, content selection, mental clarity — exactly what disappears during a panic attack.
  • No "No-Look" design: Forces reading text blocks or tapping small buttons — ignores tunnel vision and hand tremors typical of acute anxiety.
  • No crisis level detection: No distinction between mild anxiety vs. acute attack.
  • Passive-only approach: Assumes stillness = relief. Many users need to release adrenaline through gross motor movement first.
  • Too much choice: "Choose from 50 meditations." The user needs: 1 button that provides relief.

5 key insights

01
Users don't want to resolve anxiety. They want to lower the intensity right now — in this moment, before it gets worse.
02
Every existing app is built for prevention and habit formation. None is built for the peak of the crisis — the exact moment when the phone gets picked up.
03
Breathing exercises can worsen panic attacks. Body-first physical actions — stomping, squeezing, cold water — work better than meditation at peak anxiety.
04
In teenagers,anxiety happens at night. The context — dark room, racing heart, 3am — demands an aesthetic completely different from what wellness apps offer.
05
The gap between therapy sessions is where most suffering happens — and where no tool currently reaches. That's the real design opportunity.

Problem Statement

Define

After mapping all the research, I needed a single human truth to design from. I built a composite user — Sofía — not as a demographic profile but as a real behavioral portrait: the exact phone habits, the specific self-talk, the moment she decides to delete an app. Everything else was cut. If a design decision didn't answer Sofía's needs, it didn't belong in the product.

"Guide teenagers in therapy through the peak moment of an anxiety episode, when their learned coping tools feel unreachable and professional support is not available."

How might we reduce the entry friction to zero so Sofía can access help in under 3 seconds, even when her hands are shaking?
How might we guide her through the first 60 seconds of an episode without requiring her to read, decide, or think?
How might we make her feel like someone is there without requiring another human to be available?
How might we reflect her progress back to her so she sees she's gotten through episodes before?
How might we differentiate between mild anxiety and an acute episode and respond differently to each?
How might we help her therapist understand what's happening between sessions without extra effort from Sofía?
How might we make her feel like the app and her therapist are working together, not separately?
How might we create a presence that feels warm and human without pretending to be a therapist?
How might we design a character or voice that Gen Z trusts and doesn't find cringe or clinical?
How might we make the app feel like a friend who happens to know exactly what to do?
How might we make daily emotional check-ins feel like something she wants to do, not a chore?
How might we make the between-session journey feel like a game she's winning, not a treatment she's following?
How might we meet her in the exact moment she picks up her phone, before she opens TikTok?
How might we make the first 30 seconds of the experience undeniable so she doesn't delete it?
How might we design for a generation that understands mental health vocabulary but distrusts clinical aesthetics?

Design rationale

Why every decision matters

Each design choice traces back to a specific research insight.

Good design decisions feel obvious after the fact — but each one here was a deliberate choice made against an easier alternative. The dark background was a temptation to skip. The removal of streaks went against every engagement metric playbook. The wave transition adds time to a flow designed for speed. Each of these choices exists because the research demanded it, not because it was the safe option.

One screen = one action
Under high cognitive activation during an anxiety episode, users forget learned techniques and cannot perform multi-step tasks alone. Fine motor control degrades. Zero decisions at peak panic.
Resolves: "How might we guide her through the first 60 seconds without requiring her to read, decide, or think?"
Body-first instructions, not meditation
"If I focus on how I'm breathing, I realize I'm short of breath and the panic attack gets a thousand times worse." Clinical evidence confirms breathing exercises can worsen panic attacks. Physical gross motor actions — stomping, squeezing, shaking — come first.
Resolves: "How might we reduce entry friction to zero so Sofía can access help in under 3 seconds?"
Dark background — deep navy, not clinical white
"At 3 a.m., when my heart is racing at 140 bpm, hearing birds and bells makes me feel like the app is mocking me." Anxiety peaks at night. A bright morning aesthetic signals the wrong emotional register entirely.
Resolves: "How might we design for a generation that understands mental health but distrusts clinical aesthetics?"
No streak system, no score, no progress bar
App notifications reminding Sofía she missed her meditation add guilt — not motivation. A streak system turns recovery into something she can fail at. Guilt is the last emotion an anxious teenager needs.
Resolves: "How might we make the between-session journey feel like a game she's winning, not a treatment she's following?"
"The wave" transition before instructions
Users distrust generic, clinical apps. They need to feel heard before they can be guided. The wave screen — "I'm here, follow me" — closes the emotional gap between the crisis trigger and the first instruction. Empathy before action.
Resolves: "How might we make her feel like someone is there without requiring another human to be available?"

Storm mode — crisis entry

2

The second tested the exercise screen structure: character visible for emotional anchoring, one short instruction, a visible timer, and a "Next exercise" CTA. The key decision documented here — body-first instructions instead of breathing — came from the forum research.

The third wireframe mapped the home state: how does Sofía arrive here on a non-crisis day? Two card options in the middle, one prominent teal CTA at the bottom for panic mode. Accessibility — designing for crisis states

Anxiety episodes affect fine motor control, cognitive load, and visual processing. Every accessibility decision here was made with that context in mind — not as a compliance checklist, but as a direct response to what the research said about how the body behaves during a panic attack.

👁
High contrast
Dark navy background with white text meets WCAG AA at all text sizes. Teal (#00BFCA) and Lavender (#99A8F9) maintain 4.5:1+ contrast ratio for key CTAs — readable even with tunnel vision during high anxiety.
☝️
Large touch targets
All primary CTAs are minimum 56px tall. During a panic attack, fine motor control degrades — small buttons become impossible. The crisis activation button is intentionally oversized, designed for trembling hands.
🔤
Minimal reading load
No screen in the crisis flow requires reading more than 7 words at once. Text size minimum 17px. Short sentences, high line-height. Cognitive load drops sharply during an anxiety spike — the UI absorbs that cost.
🔊
Audio + text parallel
Every instruction is delivered via audio AND displayed as text simultaneously. Users in public spaces, with hearing differences, or who can't process audio during a crisis can still follow every step.
✋
No gesture-only interactions
The shake gesture and volume shortcut are optional activations — every function is also accessible via tap. Users with motor differences or who are in a context where movement isn't possible aren't locked out.
🌙
Night-mode by default
Dark UI is the only mode. Anxiety peaks at night in low-light environments — a bright white screen is physiologically jarring. No toggle needed: the default is already optimized for 3am.
Iteration history
Decision 1
Crisis screen start
The first version suggested that the user speak directly after shaking the app or pressing the volume button for quick access. However, after evaluating it in various contexts, I decided to create a visually large and eye-catching button that the user can press to speak or chat if they are with other people.
Decision 2
Mild anxiety home
On paper, I had planned three buttons one below the other because it was a manual input and not for use during a panic attack. But after rereading the research and investigating different types of anxiety peaks, I decided to change the differentiation and highlight a button with the action color that communicates a real urgency.
Decision 3
Intermediate screen
When the person taps the "I'm not okay right now" button, a micro-moment was missing between The Shore and Still Water. This would help create an app with an emotional differentiator, connect the user with Nami, and help them trust the process.
First interaction

The Onboarding

Before Nami can help, Nami needs to know you

Most apps dump you into a generic home screen. Nami does something different from the very first second: it listens. The onboarding isn't a tutorial — it's a conversation where Nami learns what makes you anxious, what has helped before, and when you see your therapist. That data becomes the foundation for every interaction that follows. The more Nami knows, the more precise the help when the episode hits.

01
Nami introduces herself
Not a terms and conditions screen. Not a feature tour. Nami speaks directly to the user in first person: "Hey, I'm Nami. I'm here for the moments when things get hard." Warm, short, zero corporate language. The user's first impression sets the trust foundation everything else is built on.
02
Nami asks your name
One field. Just your name. From this point on, Nami uses it in every interaction. "Hey, Sofía" instead of "Hey, user." Personalization begins immediately — before any feature is unlocked.
03
What makes you anxious?
Nami offers 6–8 common triggers as soft-select chips: exams, social situations, nights alone, health worries, family, uncertainty. The user picks what resonates. These become the context layer that shapes Nami's language and priority responses. Nothing clinical. Nothing overwhelming.
04
What's helped you before?
Nami asks what has worked in the past: music, movement, cold water, breathing, calling someone, distraction. This seeds the initial toolkit. The AI doesn't start from zero — it starts from what the user already knows about themselves. The first crisis response will feel familiar, not foreign.
05
When do you see your therapist?
Day and frequency. Nami sets a recurring check-in for the day after each session: "How did it go today?" Soft, no pressure. This creates the bridge between sessions and gives the user a sense that Nami and their therapist are working together — even though they're not.
06
Set up the shortcut
The final onboarding screen walks the user through setting up the widget and shake gesture. Framed not as a setup task but as "your escape hatch." The goal: the user knows before the first episode exactly how to reach Nami in under 3 seconds, without unlocking, searching, or thinking.
Why this matters

Every answer the user gives during onboarding is stored and used. The first time a crisis episode hits, Nami doesn't ask "what's wrong?" — it already knows the user's triggers, their preferred strategies, and their name. The response is immediate and personal. That's the difference between an app that feels like a stranger and one that feels like it knows you.

What it got right — and what it missed

El resultado de Stitch analizó bien las instrucciones del prompt y generó una designación system coherente con lo que había expresado — colores, jerarquía tipográfica y el tono visual oscuro estaban alineados. Sin embargo, el resultado visual no alcanzó lo que tenía en mente: la presencia del personaje no tenía la personalidad ni la calidez que Nami necesita para conectar con el usuario en un momento de crisis. Me confirmó que la dirección visual correcta requería decisiones de diseño propias, no generadas.

What comes next

Next steps

Nami resolves the current crisis—but there's a much bigger opportunity: supporting the space between therapy sessions. To get there, the next cycle begins with genuine listening.

01 — Testing
Testing with real users
Usability testing sessions with people who are experiencing or have experienced anxiety. The focus: Does Sofia, as a character, inspire trust? Does the crisis flow activate before panic escalates? Is there anything that hinders rather than helps?
02 — CLINICAL RESEARCH
Conversations with therapists
In-depth interviews with psychologists and psychiatrists to understand what happens between sessions: what they record, what they would like to know before each meeting, and what kind of digital support they would consider safe and useful for their patients.
03 — DESIGN ITERATION
Evidence-based redesign
With the lessons learned from testing and interviews, return to the flow and iterate. Prioritize real points of friction over assumed ones. Track each design change against the question: Does this reduce the distance between the difficult moment and help?
VISION FOR THE FUTURE

Nami could become the bridge between sessions — where the therapist has real context about how their patient was during the week, and the user feels that the support does not end when they close the office door.
